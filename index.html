The Antipattern

We can't deny the formative power "the fold" has in a design committee, especially for a library website, which must sometimes be built to the expectations of many stakeholders. There can only be so much on the front page above the fold ...

unless you collapse, float, and cycle through large uncompressed images with javascript.

... anything to make the committee go home, amirite?

The practice of designing for the fold treats the web as if it were print, an artifact of previous centuries. One of the repercussions is that we treat the web like a physical object, like a newspaper. In this way it seems intuitive to think that people don't scroll--which, generally, is untrue--and that the front page is the most important. For an institution that offers a lot but is at the same time very concerned with communicating just how much we offer, libraries tend to end up with very compact websites.

Moving UI

Check out this Pen!
Deadly. Accordions and carousels should show a new panel only when users ask for it. Otherwise, it should stand still and let users read the information in peace, without having the rug yanked from under them. As our user said about Siemens' big rotating box: "I didn't have time to read it. It keeps flashing too quickly." Jakob Nielsen

Sliders, carousels, slideshows, rotators - these are descriptive of function. We often set carousels to auto-rotate, to auto-slide or fade-in a new image so patrons will have the chance to see that second, third,b or fourth slide. (hit b to prevent vomiting)

In fact at the Sherman we actually spent a lot of time determining the proper number of seconds to pause before rotation. We settled on 8 seconds.

It makes sense that if people won't voluntarily click to the second or third slide, then a smoothe, unobstrusive rotation will help content in front of the user when in other cases they might not stumble on it.

The point

is that user-centric libraries must simplify. The summons for a website to be device agnostic demands a simple design. All the conventions that emerged from the fear of the fold--like carousels, complex search widgets, bloated header menus, three or four column cramped designs, font-sizes less than 16px--are detriments, and they'll be increasingly detrimental as desktops are abandoned in favor of smaller devices.

If you look around, Library Websites are designed to convince people that libraries are more than just books, that there is a place for them in the 21st century. This has meant crowding out the available screen with complex CSS grids, columns, gimmickry with jQuery, bloated drop-down menus. There is something to prove having a cool website. But these have been anything but user-centric.

Remember

  • Performance is a Feature
  • Users' capacity for cruft is rapidly diminishing.
    • Banner blindness
      • Auto-movement feels like an ad
      • Choose banner or feature text carefully. Empty content trains repeat-users to look over that element.
    • Embrace whitespace.
  • Don't sacrifice accessibility for pizzazz
  • Patrons pay for library websites.
Performance is a feature. Users bypass everything that gets in the way of the content. Anything that looks, moves, sounds, smells, or tastes like an ad is a blindspot. Embrace whitespace. Content does not have to be cramped. Users scroll. Don't sacrifice accessibility for pizzazz. The library website doesn't exist for your department heads, your colleagues, the stakeholders, or the design committee. Remember who pays the taxes / tuition.
